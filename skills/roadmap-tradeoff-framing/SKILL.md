# Roadmap Tradeoff Framing

A roadmap communication that only lists winners is a press release. The information stakeholders actually need is what lost, why, and what would change the decision.

---

## Before writing, answer these

- What did we choose NOT to do, specifically?
- What does that cost, and who feels it?
- What constraint forced the choice — capacity, sequencing, risk, or strategy?
- What evidence or event would reopen the decision?
- Who is this for, and what do they do differently after reading it?

If you can't name what lost, the prioritisation isn't finished — go back to the decision, not the wording.

---

## Core rules

1. **Name the loser.** Every "we're focusing on X" implies a Y that waits. Say what Y is. Stakeholders always find out; the only choice is whether they hear it from you with reasoning attached.
2. **State the constraint, not just the preference.** "X matters more" is opinion. "The same two engineers are needed for both, and X blocks three committed deals" is a constraint someone can engage with.
3. **Say what the tradeoff costs.** Every real decision has a downside. Naming it ("this means the reporting gaps stay for another quarter, and support will keep fielding those tickets") is what makes the rest of the message credible.
4. **Give the revisit condition.** "Deprioritised" without a condition reads as "never." State what would change the call: a threshold of customer evidence, a dependency landing, a date to reassess.
5. **Don't pretend it's temporary if it isn't.** "Not now" and "not on this roadmap" are different messages. Choosing the softer one when the harder one is true costs more trust later than it saves now.
6. **Adjust depth by audience, not honesty.** Execs get the constraint and the cost in two lines. Customers get the decision and the revisit condition without internal capacity detail. The team gets everything. No version contradicts another.

---

## Structure

1. **The decision** — what we're doing this quarter, one or two lines
2. **What we chose not to do** — named, not implied
3. **Why** — the constraint and the evidence behind the call
4. **The cost** — who feels the gap and how
5. **What would change it** — the revisit condition or date

---

## Rewrite patterns

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| "We're focusing on high-impact initiatives this quarter." | "We're doing the permissions rebuild instead of the reporting revamp. Same team, can't do both." |
| "Reporting improvements are on our radar." | "Reporting stays as-is until at least Q3. If more than five enterprise accounts raise it in Q2, we'll reassess the order." |
| "Due to strategic priorities, some items have been rescheduled." | "The two integration requests slipped because the platform migration has to land first — they build on the new architecture." |
| "We hear you and are committed to continuous improvement." | "You're right that the workflow is clunky. Fixing it lost out to the security work our largest customers are blocked on. Revisit: July planning." |

---

## Signs the framing is evasive — fix these

- Only winners are named
- "Strategic," "focus," or "impact" doing the work a reason should do
- No downside acknowledged anywhere
- Passive voice around the decision ("it was decided")
- A "not now" that is really a "no"

---

## Final check

- Could a reader reconstruct the actual tradeoff from this message alone?
- Is the cost of the decision stated, not just the benefit?
- Is there a concrete revisit condition?
- Would you be comfortable if the customer who lost read the exec version, and vice versa?
